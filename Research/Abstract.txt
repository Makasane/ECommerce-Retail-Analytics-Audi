This study develops a GIS-based prospectivity model for identifying Kuroko-type
volcanogenic massive sulfide (VMS) deposits in the Eskay Creek area, British Columbia. By
integrating geological, geochemical, and geophysical datasets, the model highlights zones
most likely to host these precious metal-rich deposits. Geochemical indicators such as barium
(Ba), manganese (Mn), zinc (Zn), silver (Ag), mercury (Hg), and antimony (Sb) were
identified as key pathfinders associated with hydrothermal mineralization. Geological
controls including andesitic volcanic and volcaniclastic host rocks, along with structural
features like dense fault networks, were found to strongly influence mineralization
localization. Geophysical data, represented by gravity and magnetic anomalies, further
refined prospectivity mapping. The combined weighted overlay approach produced a
continuous prospectivity raster that effectively discriminates high-potential areas along the
central volcanic belt. Validation against 51 known deposits yielded an area under curve
(AUC) of 0.935, confirming strong predictive performance. The model reproduces
established geological and geochemical patterns consistent with Eskay Creek-type VMS
systems and offers a practical exploration tool for targeting new deposits. Recommendations
include focused infill sampling, alteration mineral mapping, and 3D modeling to enhance
prediction accuracy. This integrated GIS methodology sets a foundation for regional
exploration and can be adapted to similar terranes worldwide.
